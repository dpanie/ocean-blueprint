Ocean chemistry

What is Ocean Acidification?

The ocean absorbs a large share of the carbon dioxide (CO₂) humans add to the atmosphere. When CO₂ dissolves into seawater, it shifts the balance of ions—raising hydrogen ions (H⁺), lowering pH, and reducing carbonate (CO₃²⁻) that many organisms need to build shells and reefs.

1) CO₂ dissolves

Extra atmospheric CO₂ moves into surface waters—especially where winds and waves mix the ocean.

2) Chemistry changes

Dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid, which increases H⁺. More H⁺ means lower pH.

3) Carbonate drops

More H⁺ reduces carbonate (CO₃²⁻), making it harder for corals and shellfish to form calcium carbonate (CaCO₃).

A healthy ocean depends on stable chemistry. Ocean acidification is a chemical shift driven by excess CO₂.

In plain language

Ocean acidification doesn’t mean the ocean becomes “acid” like vinegar. It means the ocean becomes less basic over time, and that shift changes which ions are available for life.

Why it matters chemically

The most important change isn’t just pH—it’s the loss of carbonate (CO₃²⁻). Carbonate is a building block for shells and reef structures (CaCO₃). When carbonate becomes scarce, organisms must spend more energy to build—or can’t build as well.

Key takeaway: More CO₂ → more H⁺ → lower pH → less CO₃²⁻ → weaker shell-building conditions.

The key reactions (simple and accurate)

These reactions show why additional CO₂ increases hydrogen ions (H⁺) and reduces carbonate (CO₃²⁻). You don’t need to memorize them—just follow the direction of change.

CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ CO₂ dissolves and forms carbonic acid
H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ carbonic acid releases hydrogen ions (H⁺)
HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ more H⁺ shifts balance away from carbonate
CaCO₃ + H⁺ ⇌ Ca²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ shell material can dissolve under higher H⁺ conditions

Who feels it first?

Organisms that build shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) are often among the most sensitive— especially larvae and juveniles. Changes can also ripple through food webs.

Ecosystem impacts

• Shellfish (oysters, clams, mussels): slower growth, weaker shells
• Corals: reduced reef-building over time
• Some plankton: changes in survival that affect the entire food chain

People + coastal communities

• Fisheries and aquaculture depend on healthy early life stages
• Reefs and shellfish beds support biodiversity and coastal protection
• Local economies rely on seafood, tourism, and shoreline resilience
