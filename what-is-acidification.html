What is Ocean Acidification?

Ocean acidification is a chemical chain reaction triggered by excess carbon dioxide (CO₂). When CO₂ dissolves into seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which releases hydrogen ions (H⁺). More H⁺ means a lower pH.

The real damage isn’t just “lower pH.” Those extra H⁺ ions reduce carbonate (CO₃²⁻), the building block many organisms need to make calcium carbonate shells and skeletons (oysters, clams, corals, some plankton).

Bottom line: More CO₂ → more H⁺ → less CO₃²⁻ → weaker shell-building conditions.

Key reactions

CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3
H2CO3 ⇌ H+ + HCO3−
HCO3− ⇌ H+ + CO32−
Net: CO2 + H2O ⇌ H+ + HCO3−
CaCO3 + H+ ⇌ Ca2+ + HCO3−

These equilibria shift as CO₂ rises — increasing H⁺ and reducing available CO₃²⁻.
