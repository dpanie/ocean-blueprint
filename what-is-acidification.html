Chemistry, made simple

What is Ocean Acidification?

Ocean acidification happens when the ocean absorbs extra carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the air. That CO₂ changes seawater chemistry, increasing hydrogen ions (H⁺) and making it harder for many organisms to build shells and skeletons.

The trigger

More CO₂ in the atmosphere → more CO₂ dissolving into seawater.

Cause: excess CO₂ Where: surface ocean

The chemistry shift

Dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid, which releases H⁺. More H⁺ means lower pH.

↑ H⁺ ions ↓ pH

The real consequence

Extra H⁺ reduces carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻), a key building block for calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) shells.

↓ CO₃²⁻ Harder to calcify

In one sentence

Ocean acidification is a long-term change in seawater chemistry caused by extra CO₂, leading to lower pH and less carbonate available for shell-building organisms.

Why “lower pH” isn’t the whole story

The biggest biological problem is carbonate loss (CO₃²⁻). Many organisms use carbonate to form CaCO₃. When carbonate becomes less available, shells can grow more slowly, become weaker, or even dissolve under certain conditions.

Key idea: The ocean is still slightly basic overall—but it’s becoming less basic. Small pH changes can produce meaningful chemical and biological impacts.

Key reactions (the short, useful version)

These equilibria explain why extra CO₂ increases H⁺ and reduces carbonate (CO₃²⁻). You do not need to memorize them—just understand the direction of change.

CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ CO₂ dissolves and forms carbonic acid
H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ carbonic acid releases hydrogen ions (H⁺)
HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ more H⁺ pushes the system away from carbonate
CaCO₃ + H⁺ ⇌ Ca²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ acid conditions can promote shell dissolution
Bottom line: more CO₂ → more H⁺ → lower pH → less CO₃²⁻ → tougher conditions for shell-building.

Who is affected first?

Ocean acidification doesn’t affect every organism equally. The biggest risk is for organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)—especially during early life stages.

Biological impacts

• Shellfish (oysters, clams, mussels): weaker shells, slower growth
• Corals: reduced calcification, harder reef-building
• Plankton (some species): changes in survival and food webs

Human impacts

• Fisheries and aquaculture: higher risk for shellfish hatcheries
• Coastal economies: jobs tied to seafood and tourism
• Ecosystems: reefs and shellfish beds protect shorelines and support biodiversity
